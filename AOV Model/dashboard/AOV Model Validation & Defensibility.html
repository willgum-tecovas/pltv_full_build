In plain terms. We estimate each customer's typical order size, to multiply by their expected future orders for lifetime value. Two things set it: what they buy — an exotic-boot order is worth far more than an accessory order — and how they shop — some people run a little above or below average for the same items. For customers we've barely seen, we lean on what similar customers do and let their own history take over as they order more. Everything below shows how, and proves it holds up out-of-sample.

Preface — why this approach

Why an empirical-Bayes hierarchical model?

The goal is one expected AOV per customer that we multiply by projected orders for dollar CLV. Three routes were on the table; here's why we landed on this one.

Plain machine learning

A supervised regression carries no notion of population/prior behavior, and for the ~68% one-and-done and short-history customers there's almost no per-customer signal to learn from. And there's a hard limit on how well any model can do here: a single order is mostly luck — the same customer's spend swings widely from one purchase to the next — so for someone with only one or two past orders, even a flawless model can pin down just a small slice of what they'll spend next. The rest is irreducible noise, not missing signal. Machine learning can't beat that limit; it just hides the uncertainty, where this model instead leans on what similar customers do and shrinks toward it.

Gamma-Gamma (the standard CLV spend model)

The Fader–Hardie Gamma-Gamma model is the usual monetary-value component in customer-base analysis, but its hyperparameters didn't fit our data. It assumes spend is gamma-distributed around a gamma-distributed customer mean, independent of purchase frequency and sharing one shape parameter — assumptions our order-value distribution violates (heavy department and exotic structure), so the fit didn't hold.

What we built

An empirical-Bayes hierarchical (credibility) model that does two things the alternatives don't. First, it shrinks each customer toward a population prior by exactly how much we've observed — thin histories lean on the population, loyal customers on their own data — which is the provably most-accurate move with noisy short histories. Second, it explicitly prices the features that drive order value, merchandise department and exotic leather, at the order level, separating "the premium for what they bought" from "who they are." That feature-aware pricing is the piece we specifically wanted, and neither ML nor Gamma-Gamma delivered it.

Why it's solvable

2 · Most “customer noise” is really department + exotic

Within a customer, the spend that looks erratic is mostly just basket mix — an accessory one trip, boots the next. An order-weighted decomposition pulls that structure out of the noise: 77% of within-customer variance is department + exotic, leaving just 23% as genuine order-to-order noise (the clean within-cell σ = 0.53 in log terms). That small, clean residual is exactly what lets a customer's own orders carry real weight instead of being drowned out.

How the number is built — the math

3 · From structure to a single dollar figure

Three pieces turn the variance above into one AOV per customer: how much to trust a customer's own orders (the credibility weight), how to regularize a thin purchase mix (mix-shrinkage), and how they assemble into expected AOV.

Credibility weight — the shrunk baseline θ̂c

Combine two Normal sources — the customer's own orders (noisy, spread σ) and their group (spread τ) — weighting each by its precision. K = σ2/τ2 is how many orders it takes for a customer's own data to count as much as the group; it isn't chosen, it falls out of the two spreads.

K = σ2τ2     wc = ncnc + K     θ̂c = wc θcraw + (1 − wc)(μ+Xβ)
the shrunk baseline = (weight on the customer) × their data + (weight on the group) × the prior
What the baseline θ means — and why baselines look alike. It is not a customer's raw average order value. It's that average with “what they bought” priced out: for each order we first subtract that order's department/exotic premium (γ), then average. So a $550 exotic-boot buyer and a $120 accessory buyer can share the same baseline — the model reads them as “equally high spenders for what they buy,” and prices the boot-vs-accessory gap separately through γ. That's exactly why baselines come out far more alike than raw order values (which span ~7×): the big department-driven differences are removed, leaving only the genuine person-to-person spread τ (≈ ±27% at one standard deviation). Department and exotic aren't ignored — paying for them here and in γ would double-count, so they live only at the order level.

Mix-shrinkage — the shrunk purchase mix p̂c

A one-order customer's observed mix is “100% whatever they bought once,” which is overconfident. Credit them with M pseudo-orders spread like the population, so a thin history can't look too certain (a cell = one department × exotic-or-not).

p̂c(cell) = nc(cell) + M · ppop(cell)nc + M
their orders in the cell, plus M pseudo-orders' worth of the population, over their total orders plus M

Expected AOV — the assembled formula

The dollar value of an order in each cell (baseline × the cell's premium × the mean-correction), averaged over the shrunk mix — which, because the mix is linear, collapses to one pass over the data.

E[AOVc] = ∑cells p̂c(cell) · eθ̂c + γcell + σ2/2
= eθ̂cnc + M ( ∑their cells nc(cell) Acell  +  M Āpop ),    Acell = eγcell + σ2/2
the +σ2/2 is the median→mean correction; in practice an empirical smearing factor replaces it (notebook §4)

The payoff — defensibility

5 · Out-of-sample accuracy vs. the baselines

Median absolute % error against realized AOV in a temporal holdout, by number of training orders. The EB model beats the customer's own raw mean everywhere, and beats the flat population average from 3 orders on. At 1–2 orders it essentially ties the population mean (35.6% vs 35.0%): there's almost no per-customer signal that thin, so the model correctly falls back toward the population rather than chasing noise. Lower is better.

6 · And it's unbiased in aggregate

Customers binned by predicted AOV; each point is a bin's mean prediction versus its mean realized value. On the dashed line is perfect calibration. The bins track it closely and sit fractionally below — a slight, deliberate under-prediction (aggregate ratio 0.986). Accurate per bin and unbiased in total is exactly what PLTV needs when these AOVs are multiplied by projected orders and summed.
